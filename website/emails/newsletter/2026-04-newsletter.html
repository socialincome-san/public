Field Notes

To keep you in the loop, this is how our programs are running:

  • In Sierra Leone 🇸🇱, we now run seven programs. Our core program plus six thematic ones: cash for Ebola survivors, widows, cacao farmers, women in skills training or financial literacy courses, and girls who survived domestic violence. All living in poverty.

  • In Liberia 🇱🇷, we've found our first local program partner. With Help a Mother and Newborn Initiative, we're moving from pilot to program. In other words: three years of support for mothers in poverty, starting from early motherhood. What does the evidence say about such programs? Nature tells you.

  • Two volunteers are on their way to Ghana 🇬🇭 to set up the country office with Kwame and assess two local NGOs for a fast start. How long until we're operational in a new country? It depends, mostly on bureaucracy. In Ghana, about eight weeks to get all permits and registrations.

  • 🌐 Our volunteer team has two new additions, so new they're not even on the team page yet. Paula will help us stay organized and motivated, Hendrik will make sure our finances stay transparent as we add new countries.

Working trap

Would you quit your job if your basic needs were met? Research consistently shows that participants in UBI pilots don't drop out of the labor force. Yes, some reduce their hours slightly, but for good reasons: going back to school, cutting overtime, or stepping away from poorly paid, exhausting work that offers little in return.

People don't get lazy. They get options.

Something big

A twitter dude with a lot of followers wrote a blog post that went viral last month, arguing that AI hit a turning point in early 2026: because programming was automated, AI can now accelerate its own development across every other field. Because of this essay, stock market lost billions within days.

If he's right, UBI isn't a nice idea for later. It's urgent.

My World in Data

2.3 billion

That’s how many people use mobile money today, making fast cash transfers possible nearly anywhere. It's the infrastructure that lets a donation in Zurich reach a mother in Monrovia within seconds.

Sometimes the most radical thing is a simple transfer.

960 million

Around 960 million Africans have mobile coverage but don't use mobile internet. The problem isn't signal, it's affordability. That's why $40 4G smartphones are now being offered across several African countries.

Sometimes the strongest signal is a lower price tag.

150 hours

That's how much time the average person spends each year sending and receiving voice notes. Yes, those nerve-racking messages that are often so long you've forgotten the beginning by the time they end.

The good news: 55% of voice notes go unplayed.
