Trees that feed

Our newest program supports 100 cocoa farmers living in poverty who face shrinking harvests from aging trees and climate change. The best fix is to cut old trees, plant stronger ones, and wait 2–3 years for better yields. But if you’re struggling to survive, you can’t afford to go without income while waiting for new trees to grow. Social Income helps farmers bridge those years so they can replant now and secure stronger harvests in the future.

Made possible by EU funding.

Unemployed geniuses

Scientists who study UBI pilots keep repeating the same finding— people don’t drop out of the workforce just because they receive a minimum income. This is also the case for Evelyn Forget, but on the Freakonomics podcast about UBI, she included an interesting factoid:

“If you look at the 18th and 19th century, some of the great scientific breakthroughs and cultural breakthroughs were made by people who did not work.”

Evelyn Forget

Canadian Economist

Hell-o

This column made me laugh because it captures the chaos of online meetings. Someone joins before the meeting starts and says hello to the people waiting. Everyone fumbles to unmute and say hello back. Then someone dares to ask a “How is everyone?” that is met with silence or, at best, a stuttered “fine.” Another person joins, and the comedy begins again. The fix is easy: once three or more people are already there, don’t say a word. Just wave.

One wave to rule them all.

No more billionaires

In her new book, Ingrid Robeyns argues that no one deserves to be a billionaire, not even you. Every billionaire, she says, is a policy failure. She proposes not only a poverty line but also a wealth line. No time to read the book? Listen to her arguments in this podcast, or take it from Richard G. Wilkinson:

“The best case I've read for putting an upper limit on the accumulation of wealth. Even the super-rich might be glad if there was a finishing line!”

Richard G. Wilkinson

British social epidemiologist

My World in Data

50%

I know cash transfers help families eat better, but a new study in rural Kenya showed that a one-time $1,000 transfer cut infant deaths nearly in half. The biggest gains came when money arrived around birth, nudging more mothers into hospitals for delivery.

Cash, at the right time, is the quickest way to save lives.

18%

If you thought that cash transfers only impact infant development in low-income countries, think again. A population-wide study in Michigan found that cash transfers cut pre-term births by up to 18% and low birthweight births by up to 27%.

Apparently storks take cash.
