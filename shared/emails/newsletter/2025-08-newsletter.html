Pangolins, Parties and Players. Hi {{ insert first_name 'default=there' }}.

Marc and I were theoretically on holiday in Liberia this month. Our out-of-office messages painted a picture of sun and cocktails, but in reality we were on the ground in Monrovia setting up a cash transfer pilot.

Here’s a recap of the adventure, followed by the latest updates.

My Week in Liberia

  • Wednesday: I was told that speaking Koloqua, Liberia’s creole, is as easy as adding an “o” to the end of a sentence. “Good day-o,” “It’s free-o” and “Bye-o”. Sounds simple, but as it turns out there is a lot more rhythm and difficulty to it. But hey, I tried-o!
  • Thursday: Trump put Liberia in the headlines by forgetting their official language and asking their president, “Where did you learn English so well?”. Locals had a laugh: “At least the world’s talking about us now.”
  • Friday: The NGOs we met gave real meaning to the old university buzzphrase grassroots engagement. One young team is tackling infant mortality by partnering with traditional midwives while encouraging young mothers to give birth in hospitals.
  • Saturday: We had a drink at a bar with a sign that read: “No employee is allowed to sit or sleep on the chair.” Safe to say that the owner probably isn’t winning any awards for workers’ rights.
  • Sunday: The city slows down as people head to church as most Liberians are Christian. Afterward, crowds gather at Unification Square, a former execution site from 1980 turned public park. It now stands as a symbol of reconciliation and national unity.
  • Monday: We learned that pangolins are the world’s most trafficked mammal. At the national sanctuary outside Monrovia, staff don’t feed them but instead take them on walks so they can hunt their own insects. Ever seen a pangolin on a leash? Me neither, but I’m having fun picturing a tiny one on a pink leash.
  • Tuesday: We found out that kids graduating from kindergarten here get full-on graduation parties, and honestly, they’re onto something. Why not celebrate small wins? Every milestone’s a good excuse for music, cake, and pride.
 

Just a few mini-stories from our time in Monrovia that felt worth sharing.
